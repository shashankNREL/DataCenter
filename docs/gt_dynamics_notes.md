Studying an islanded GE LM2500 aeroderivative gas turbine supplying an AI data center is a brilliant and highly relevant engineering problem. Because AI data centers generate severe step-load variations (the "sawtooth" pattern during ML training synchronization), an islanded system becomes an extreme test of frequency stability and governor loop speed.
Here is a breakdown of how the LM2500's physical mechanics and governor control loop react to the millisecond-scale transience generated by your S3 dataset.
1. Physical Strengths & Vulnerabilities of the LM2500 in Island Mode
Aeroderivatives are based on flight engines (the LM2500 is derived from the CF6 aircraft engine). This gives them a distinct mechanical footprint compared to heavy-duty industrial turbines.  
MTU Aero Engines
The Advantage: Low Low-Pressure Rotor Inertia
The LM2500 is a two-spool (split-shaft) engine.
The Gas Generator (GG) spool (High-Pressure Compressor + High-Pressure Turbine) is aerodynamically coupled—not mechanically locked—to the Power Turbine (PT) spool which drives the electrical generator.  
MTU Aero Engines
Because the PT spool has relatively low physical inertia, it can spin up and accept fuel-driven energy changes much faster than a massive single-shaft heavy-duty turbine. An LM2500 can ramp comfortably at 20–30 MW per minute in standard grid-following applications and can handle sudden block load jumps of up to 25%–30% of its rated capacity in an islanded state without tripping.
The Bottleneck: The Aerodynamic Coupling Lag
When your AI dataset registers a massive power spike (e.g., 10 MW in 200 ms):
The governor opens the fuel valve.
The high-pressure gas generator must suck in more air, compress it, combustion temperature spikes, and the GG accelerates.
This increased mass flow of exhaust gas expands across the Power Turbine to finally change the electrical output.
This thermodynamic "fill time" introduces a 0.5 to 1.5-second transport delay between fuel valve movement and actual electrical torque output.
2. How the Governor Reacts to AI Transience
A typical modern digital governor for an LM2500 (like a Woodward MicroNet or GE Mark VIe) relies on an architecture governed by IEEE standard models (e.g., GAST or GGOV1 models).
When the 100ms step-change from your Python data hits the generator, the system goes through three distinct behavioral phases:
Phase 1: The Inertial Response (0 to 100ms)
The governor cannot react at the 100ms scale due to actuator limits.
As the AI load instantly spikes, the extra energy is stolen directly from the kinetic energy stored in the rotating mass of the generator and Power Turbine.

Phase 2: Governor Transient Overshoot (100ms to 2 seconds)
The governor detects the frequency deviation via its speed sensors. It switches from standard steady-state Droop Control into its Transient Reset/Isolating (Isochronous) PID loop.
The Action: The fuel metering valve commands a rapid opening stroke.
The Danger: Because of the thermodynamic transport delay, the frequency will continue to drop even as fuel increases, reaching its lowest point (Frequency Nadir). If the nadir drops below standard threshold protections (typically 57.5 Hz on a 60 Hz system), the turbine will trigger an under-frequency trip, blacking out the data center.
Phase 3: The Recovery Phase (2 to 5 seconds)
As the gas generator catches up with the fuel command, power output surges, arresting the frequency drop and bringing it back to nominal (60 Hz).
The Counter-Problem (Load Rejection): When the AI training step ends, the dataset will show power plunging instantly. The stored thermal energy in the turbine will cause a sudden frequency overshoot before the governor can successfully choke the fuel valve back down.
